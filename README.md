# SVB-Interactive-Bond-Simulator

The written analysis in this project is my own — the research, the argument, and the personal context that frames it. The interactive simulator was built with the assistance of Claude, Anthropic's AI, which helped translate the financial concepts explored in this paper into a working, visual model. I wanted to be transparent about that distinction. The goal was never to obscure how the tool was made, but to make the ideas more accessible than a written explanation alone could achieve. 

When my family fled Vietnam after the war, the majority of their net worth was carried in gold and jewels. After the Communist Party of Vietnam had seized power, ethnic Chinese were targeted and harassed for their assets. Shovels and sledgehammers were used by government officials to tear apart gardens and plastered walls in search of hidden capital. Anything that had been in any bank, was now spending power for the government. Officials could be paid off. The militia was corrupt.  There was no such concept as safekeeping for money. Unless it was tangible, value was nothing more than a daydream. My family’s story of safekeeping wealth came also with the molestation and torment by Thai and Cambodian pirates. Silicon Valley Bank's collapse, decades removed and worlds apart, is a reminder that the safety of money has never been as guaranteed as we are led to believe.

This project is an interactive simulator that models how the interest rate environment affected the assets of Silicon Valley Bank. The assets analyzed in this project are their government-backed bonds, and how the various classifications of those holdings created losses too significant to absorb. Beyond the bond portfolio, a concentrated depositor base, the ease of digital banking, and negative media coverage all accelerated their collapse. Although the bank run took only three days to fully liquidate SVB, this situation had been years in the making — originating with the aggressive rate cuts that followed the onset of COVID-19.

When COVID-19 struck, the world had shut itself down almost overnight. A novel virus with no cure in sight had already begun claiming lives, and the widespread fear it generated brought economic activity to a near-standstill. With no one spending, progress halted — innovation, commerce, and growth all froze in place. In response to this, The Federal Reserve slashed interest rates as a way to incentivize borrowing, which in turn would stimulate investment into the economy. It worked, many economic sectors began slowly to fundraise and pull money out of the banking system, in particular, technology. As investments in technology began yielding outsized returns, venture capitalists and tech startups found themselves flush with capital — and in need of a trusted institution to hold it. Silicon Valley Bank was perfectly positioned, with the credibility, location, and relationships to attract large deposits from the very firms driving the boom.

When a bank receives deposits, the cash comes under its safekeeping — but it remains a liability. That money belongs to the depositor and must stay liquid enough to be returned on demand. To put this now idle capital to work, banks invest in securities known as bonds. Backed by the United States government, bonds are considered a risk-free investment. When deposit capital is invested in this manner, the bank is guaranteed the return of its principal along with recurring interest payments it can keep as income as long as it holds the bond until maturity.

Bond yields and the broader rate environment are closely linked. When rates are low — as they were during the COVID era — bond yields follow suit. With deposit capital on hand, SVB did what any prudent bank would do: it invested in government-backed securities. The problem was timing. By locking in during a period of historically low rates, SVB locked in weak returns on its bond portfolio — returns that would soon be undermined as the rate environment shifted. Meanwhile, the interest it owed depositors on savings accounts would rise alongside rates, creating a widening gap between what SVB earned and what it owed.

After investing in bonds, a bank must classify each holding under one of three categories: held-to-maturity, available-for-sale, or trading securities. HTM, or held-to-maturity, bonds are securities the bank intends to hold through their full term. Because the bank has no plans to sell, unrealized gains or losses are not reflected on the balance sheet — what matters is that at maturity, the bond returns its full principal along with the agreed-upon interest. AFS, or available-for-sale, bonds offer more flexibility. These can be liquidated if the bank needs capital quickly. Unlike HTM holdings, AFS bonds are carried at fair market value — meaning their worth is continuously marked to the current rate environment. Any unrealized gains or losses flow directly onto the balance sheet, visible to regulators, investors, and the public alike. Trading securities are a separate category entirely — short-term investments purchased with the explicit intention of profiting from price movements rather than holding for income.

As COVID-19 restrictions lifted, inflation took hold across the economy — prices rose, and the Federal Reserve responded by doing the opposite of what it had done during the pandemic: it hiked interest rates aggressively to cool things down.The consequences for SVB's bond portfolio were immediate. As rates climbed, the fair market value of their existing bonds fell. Newer bonds were now hitting the market with far more attractive yields, making SVB's low-rate holdings look increasingly unfavorable by comparison. In addition to that, the pressure compounded on the liability side as well. Savings account yields, unlike bond returns, move with the rate environment. As rates rose, SVB was obligated to pay depositors — including its venture capital and tech startup clients — progressively higher returns on their cash. The bank was caught in a widening squeeze: earning modest, locked-in returns on its bond portfolio while paying out ever-increasing yields to the very depositors whose money had funded those bonds in the first place.

This is where the internal structure of Silicon Valley Bank began to crack. With interest rates now elevated, borrowing had become expensive and venture capital funding had dried up. Starved of fresh investment, SVB's clients — founders and venture capitalists alike — turned to their deposits to keep operations running. Withdrawals began to mount. To meet the demand for liquid capital, SVB started selling off its AFS securities. After liquidating approximately $21 billion worth, the bank reported a realized loss of nearly $1.8 billion. What followed made things significantly worse. Alongside the loss disclosure, SVB announced it was seeking to raise an additional $2 billion in capital to cover continued withdrawal pressure. The message, however unintentionally, was clear: the bank was stretched. For depositors, the announcement was alarming — and the reason runs deeper than surface-level panic. The FDIC, the federal body responsible for insuring deposits at American financial institutions, guarantees accounts only up to $250,000. The vast majority of SVB's clientele — technology startups and venture-backed firms carrying operating capital, payroll reserves, and fundraising proceeds — held balances that dwarfed that threshold. If SVB failed, anything above the insured limit was at risk of being lost entirely. For many of these companies, that meant everything.

Once fear took hold among even a handful of depositors, it was nearly over for Silicon Valley Bank. What made the situation uniquely dire was the nature of its clientele. With its depositor base so heavily concentrated within a single sector, SVB's clients were deeply interconnected — the same venture capital firms sat on the boards of the same startups, all banking at the same institution. When concern began circulating through that network, it spread fast.

Once the story reached headlines, there was little SVB could do but watch. Digital banking removed every obstacle that once slowed a bank run. No lines, no waiting — just a few taps on a phone. In a single day, depositors demanded over $42 billion in withdrawals, the largest single-day bank run in American history. The fear was not irrational. SVB had already disclosed a $1.8 billion realized loss on its $26 billion AFS portfolio. But what unnerved investors more was what remained unseen. Behind the $91 billion HTM portfolio sat a mountain of unrealized losses that had never touched the accounting books. No write-downs, no disclosures, no public reckoning — just a number that no one outside the bank could accurately calculate. Nobody knew just how deep the water was. And in that uncertainty, the only rational move for a depositor was to get out. Two days after the demanded $42 billion dollar withdrawal, Silicon Valley Bank was seized by the FDIC and deemed a failure.

Silicon Valley Bank did not fail because it broke the rules. It took depositor capital and put it to work. It classified its bonds correctly. It followed banking regulation to the letter. And yet it still fell. That is what makes this case so striking. SVB was not brought down by fraud or negligence — it was brought down by timing, concentration, and circumstance. A rate environment that shifted faster than any model predicted. A depositor base so tightly networked that fear traveled through it like a current. A media cycle that amplified panic before the bank had any chance to respond. And a digital infrastructure that turned a slow-moving crisis into a 48-hour collapse. My family did not lose everything because they were careless. They lost it because the system they existed within failed them — swiftly, completely, and without warning. The militia came. The banks were seized. The value they had built evaporated overnight. What remained was only what they could carry. Silicon Valley Bank's depositors were not carrying gold. They were carrying payroll, operating capital, and the financial futures of the companies they had spent years building. The mechanism was different. The feeling, I imagine, was not.
