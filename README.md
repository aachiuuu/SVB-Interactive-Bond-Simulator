# SVB-Interactive-Bond-Simulator
America is among one of the top global powerhouses in the world. And as a direct result of this, many countries look to the nation as a big brother. Decisions made by the American financial system are closely monitored by a large multitude of countries to see how they can profit or in other circumstances, steer clear of what is happening. One of the most important concepts in the American economy are interest rates and why they fluctuate. In its most basic definition, interest is the price you pay to borrow someone else's money. The best way to introduce the concept and how it affects the economy is to show the impact on a typical consumer. 

When interest rates go up; mortgages, car payments, credit card APR & loans all do too. Inversely, when interest rates go down, so does all of the aforementioned list. It is important to note that when borrowing money, interest rates are negotiated through contract. So securing a loan or car payment may have an already locked in interest rate that shares no correlation to the fluctuations. If a mortgage payment shoots up, spending may be deferred to focus on fulfilling that. If you are looking to purchase a car and are told interest rates are high. It would be less than ideal to purchase because you will be paying a rate to the lender which is higher than what it could be.

There is no one governmental department that sets interest rates flat out for every category. Rather, it starts with the Federal Reserve and how they set the overnight lending rate, which is the rate which financial institutions use to borrow from each other. And it is influenced by a multitude of factors ranging from; inflation to unemployment, all the way to war. "This overnight rate — the price at which financial institutions lend to each other — is called the Federal Funds Rate, and it serves as the foundation from which all other borrowing costs in the economy are derived." Although there is no committee that sets each category of interest rates, there is a body of 12 voters who discuss and decide how to modify the Federal Funds Rate based on information gathered between meetings. The committee that oversees this is the Federal Open Market Committee (FOMC). As mentioned, the committee has 12 voting members but is composed of all 7 Federal Reserve Board Governors plus 12 regional Federal Reserve Bank presidents. Of the 12 regional presidents, only 5 are allowed to vote at a time in addition to the 7 Board governors. 

What is important for this project is to analyze the relationship between interest rates and bond prices. Government Backed Bonds are a type of investment that guarantees the return of your principal if held to maturity, and pays the investor regular interest along the way. When interest rates rise, newly issued bonds offer higher yields — making existing bonds, locked in at lower rates, less attractive. As a result, the market price of those existing bonds fall. Rising rates are good for new savers, but damaging to anyone holding older bonds. Lowered rates are good for borrowers but have little incentive for savers. The bonds focused on in this project are government-issued securities. There are a variety of government bonds, the government-issued securities relevant to this analysis are T-Bills, T-Notes, and T-Bonds. The only distinction between them is time — T-Bills mature in under a year, T-Notes in one to ten years, and T-Bonds in ten to thirty years. Duration matters enormously in a rising rate environment — the longer the maturity, the greater the price decline when rates rise

This project argues that SVB's collapse was not the result of bad investments — every security they held was government-backed and credit-risk free — but rather a catastrophic failure of duration management, compounded by a uniquely concentrated depositor base and the fastest interest rate hiking cycle in four decades.
